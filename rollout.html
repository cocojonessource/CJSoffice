Rollout Review

Body So Tea is tea… but that’s not what we’re talking about.

This is about the world around it. What people understood, where things started getting confusing, and what the rollout actually felt like to follow.

Here we go (uh oh)

Before I start my word vomit, because you know I’m a yapper, let me just clarify something.

I don’t just look at one thing in one moment just because I like it.

If I like something, it actually makes me want to understand it more.

So when I look at things, I’m not trying to pick it apart, and I’m not coming from a place of nothing is ever enough (it’s never too much, never too much).

Your girl just naturally thinks about growth, longevity, and cohesiveness. I’ve said that before.

And I’m not trying to be your biggest music critic and tell you if I like a song or not, because I do like Body So Tea.

I’m just looking at the world that was built around it.

“

I would never tell you what type of music to make, but I will always look at how what you make connects and builds.

The fragmented rollout

What you didn’t tell us

When the first video dropped and it felt like you were being vulnerable and opening up to us, there was immediately a lot of discourse.

Not just about what you were saying, but about the direction you should take.

And that was already a hot topic before this video.

But now it was layered with you asking the question yourself.

Trying to figure out where you go next, and why it feels like you’re almost there but not fully clicking.

So when you put that question out there, the entire internet answered.

And whether intentional or not, that immediately set the expectation that by the end of this… there would be an answer.

It was presented like we were about to go on a journey with you.

Like we were going to figure it out together.

Like we were back in it as a team.

But it didn’t really work that way.

Because it was never actually a conversation.

There was no engagement with your fans or the audience for advice.

You had people in your comments genuinely trying to help, trying to respond, trying to guide — whether they were right or wrong.

You had other artists opening up and sharing their own struggles.

And there was no real response to any of it.

Just silence.

Then the second video came.

And that’s when it clicked.

As a fan, it no longer felt like something we were experiencing with you.

It felt like a rollout.

And once that shift happens, the authenticity people thought they were stepping into starts to feel different.

The other side of it

The Other Side of It

What Really Happened

I had to check myself at one point.

Because I started getting annoyed at how people were talking about you.

And I had to ask myself…

why am I getting upset at people critiquing you or giving advice I don’t agree with…

when I critique you too?

Like… you’re literally reading this on an app I built just to get my thoughts out in a more digestible way.

So clearly I’m not against critique.

So I had to actually look at what was going on instead of just reacting to it.

And that’s when it clicked for me.

The disconnect wasn’t random.

It came from how everything was being presented.

Because you’ve been saying for years that you have different sides to you.

And now I finally understand what that means.

It’s not a “new Coco” every time.

It’s you expanding.

Trying different sounds, different expressions, different parts of yourself.

But that’s not how it was being received.

Because the way everything is presented, every moment feels like a decision.

Every song feels like:

this is who she is now.

So instead of people seeing growth, they’re reacting like you’re choosing a direction.

And once you frame it like that, people are going to respond strongly.

And that’s where the audience split comes in.

Because different people are attached to different versions of you.

You have people who found you through ICU.

And to them, that wasn’t just a song.

That was something they felt was missing in R&B.

So they’re protective of that.

It feels rare to them.

So when you move differently, it doesn’t feel like growth.

It feels like they’re losing something.

And at the same time, you have other people who are ready to see more.

Different sounds, different energy, something bigger.

So now you have multiple audiences reacting to the same moment in completely different ways.

And the rollout didn’t connect that.

It didn’t show that all of these sides still belong to the same person.

So now every move feels separate.

Every sound feels like a shift instead of a step.

And that’s where the disconnect really is.

Fan Fatigue

This is when I knew something was off

This is when I really noticed that the rollout was a mess.

Not just to casual listeners, but to your fanbase. Your core.

The fanbase became passive.

And what I mean by that is…

instead of it being “yay, Coco posted,”
it became:

check the notification…
sigh
it’s one of those videos.

The conversation had already ran its course after the second video.

And people didn’t even watch the rest fully.

To the point where in the last video, there was a snippet people completely missed.

And that’s why I said something about it.

Because I realized… dang, y’all aren’t even watching these all the way through anymore.

Literally, the reactions were all like:

I love her.
I support her.
I for real don’t know what’s going on right now.

Or it was just:

I liked it.
I retweeted it.
and I closed the app.

And that says a lot about how the rollout connected to your fans.

Because even the fan you retweeted — I don’t know if it was you or your team —

when she said,
“girl, this better be the last video”

that is literally how the entire base was feeling.

And we still love you.

It just didn’t feel like an open conversation where we were included anymore.

But we’re still going to support you, even if the engagement dropped on our side.

The Wrong Conversation

The rollout and the song weren’t asking the same thing

At a certain point, the conversation stopped being about the song.

Body So Tea did not need the level of depth that the rollout gave it.

The rollout was asking people to think about your identity, your direction, and your future.

The song was just asking to be experienced.

And those are two completely different conversations.

So instead of people reacting to the song for what it was…

they were trying to figure out what it meant for you as an artist.

That’s why everything felt so heavy.

Because the documentary and the song were not asking the audience to think about the same thing.

And once those two things are misaligned, the conversation is already off.

The Paid Campaign

So where was all this content coming from?

I was suspicious before I actually knew what was going on.

Because by this point, we were already fatigued with these clips. Then, all of a sudden, I started seeing even more of them being posted by random accounts.

Some of them were Coco-focused. Some of them had nothing to do with you. But they were all posting the same videos and spamming the clips everywhere.

Some of the clips we had already seen.

But some of them, we had never seen before.

And I like to think I’m pretty tuned in to the Coco side of the interwebs, so naturally I’m sitting there like:

Where is all this content coming from?

Why do these random pages have clips that your fans have not seen?

Why have these clips not been posted on your account?

What is the source?

Because there wasn’t one. The footage was just popping up on random accounts with no explanation.

So I started looking into it.

At first, I found a new account using your picture and posting clips from the documentary. Then I found other small and random accounts posting different parts of the same conversation. Again, some of the clips had already been posted, but some of the footage had never been posted officially at all.

And it kept happening.

Multiple accounts. The same videos. The same conversation being spammed over and over again.

Then I found out it actually was a paid clip campaign.

People were being given footage and instructions on how to frame it. The brief told them to make the clips feel like a documentary about an artist at a crossroads. It pushed the conversations about the label, the doubt, finding a new sound, stepping into a new era and connecting all of that back to Body So Tea.

So the heaviness of the rollout was not accidental.

This was the conversation the campaign was intentionally telling people to keep pushing.

And I personally do not think that was the right conversation to platform this heavily.

I understand that I am not in the rooms where these decisions are being made, but this is another one where I genuinely do not understand the decision. Your fans were already fatigued with this conversation, and then the paid strategy was to have random accounts continue spamming the same clips and the same narrative.

I don’t think that was the right strategy for this rollout at all.

And because the payment was based on views, of course people were going to keep posting. They were going to use the footage, repeat clips and package them however they thought would get engagement.

But what it actually did was keep a conversation going that had already divided your fan base and the general public.

There is also the smaller part where I found a performance clip I had posted sitting inside the Week Two campaign files. I recognized the downloader filename because it was the same one I used.

I’m not claiming ownership over a public performance. That is not my point.

It was just another moment where I was like:

Oh, okay. So this is happening again with your team and me.

I had already created my own Body So Tea fan engagement idea for people to participate in and post across social media. But after finding the campaign and seeing how everything was being pushed, I hit my own point of fatigue with the rollout.

I was like:

Yeah. Y’all got this.

Not saying that you need me for anything.

But I stopped wanting to do too much beyond posting and reposting what we already had, because the team clearly had the direction they wanted to take.

And then there was the AI.

✧ throws hands up ✧

I’m not going to try to understand or explain your personal stance on generative AI, how deeply you dislike it or what parts of it you may be okay with. That is a tricky conversation, and I would rather not speak for you.

But before people know any of that nuance, the common assumption is that artists would be against generative AI because they want to protect their own image, voice and likeness.

And some of these accounts were creating AI images of you for this rollout.

I personally do not think that was necessary.

Especially because there had already been a little commotion when AI was used during the Christmas rollout.

So now, on top of everything else, somebody messier could have found this and turned it into:

“Coco Jones is paying people to post her clips.”

“Coco Jones is paying people to create AI images of her.”

And once it leaves the label and reaches the internet, it does not become:

“Her label ran a paid clip campaign.”

It becomes your name.

They drag Coco Jones, even if paid campaigns are a normal strategy labels use.

That is why I think the strategy was risky. Not because paid campaigns exist, but because this specific one kept spamming a conversation people were already tired of, added AI images that were not needed and created room for your name to get dragged if the wrong person found it first.

What Could Have Been

This idea could've worked.

I don't think this was a bad idea.

I actually think there was something here.

I just don't think Body So Tea was the song for this version of it.

Because now the song has to carry this whole conversation about your career, your direction, your sound... and I just don't think those two things matched.

To me, Body So Tea should've just been allowed to be fun.

You could've still done the documentary.

I just think it should've been about Body So Tea.

Show us hearing the song for the first time.

Show us you recording it.

Talk about why you liked it.

Talk about changing the verses.

Show behind the scenes from the photoshoot.

Show behind the scenes from the video.

Do your Get Ready With Me's using the song before it comes out.

Use it as background music instead of always presenting it as, "Here's my new single."

That's how people start making their own videos to it.

It starts feeling like they found the song instead of the song being handed to them.

And honestly...

I still think you can turn this around.

The stuff you've been posting after the release?

The dances.

The energy.

Just having fun with it.

That's the world I wish we would've gotten from the beginning.

Because that feels like Body So Tea.

The documentary idea doesn't have to disappear either.

I actually think it could be really cool for CJ Two.

Just film throughout the process.

Then when everything is over, look back and tell the story.

Not just from your perspective, but from ours too.

How did the fans feel?

What were we seeing?

What worked?

What didn't?

I think that would've been a really interesting documentary.
