Rollout Review

The song was never the only thing I was looking at.

I was looking at how we got there, what people understood, where they started getting lost and what the full experience felt like from the audience side.

The lens

Before I get into the rollout, I want to explain how I am looking at it.

I do not really look at things as one isolated moment. Even when something is good, my mind automatically goes to what it connects to, what it builds into and how it is going to play out later. I am always thinking, okay, this worked, but how did it work? What did people understand from it? What happens after this?

So this is not me trying to find something wrong with everything or saying nothing is ever enough. I just think about expansion and longevity. I look at the process, not only the result.

And for this meeting, I am not reviewing whether I like the music. I do like the music. I am looking at everything around it: the story, the timing, the way it moved across platforms, what the audience was being asked to follow and whether all of those pieces felt connected.

“

I do not try to tell you what to make. I think about how what you make connects, grows and lasts.

I also want to be clear that this is not me saying I know every conversation happening behind the scenes. I do not know every limitation, every deadline or every decision that had to be made.

This is simply what the finished experience communicated to me as someone who followed it closely and was watching how other people were responding too.

First, the record

What happened, where it happened and what people received.

Before I explain how I felt, I want the rollout placed in order. Not because every post needs to be overanalyzed, but because the timing matters when we are talking about how the full story was understood.

Why the timeline matters

When everything is separated into individual posts, each piece can look fine on its own. But once it is placed in order, you can see what questions were introduced, how long people had to sit with them and whether the next piece actually continued that conversation.

This is also where the platform differences become easier to see, because not every part of the timeline reached the same people.

Following along

I did not start this rollout already looking for a problem.

I was excited. The disconnect became noticeable while I was trying to follow what the rollout was asking us to understand.

Where the disconnect started

The first conversation made it feel like we were about to get a deeper look into your artistry—where you felt stuck, what you were trying to figure out and where you wanted to go next.

So naturally, I started watching the rollout through that lens. I was waiting for the pieces to keep building on each other. But instead of feeling like one full conversation, it started feeling like pieces of a conversation being released around another campaign.

And I think that is where my excitement started turning into confusion. Not because I did not care about what was being said, but because I cared and wanted to understand how it all connected.

The bridge already felt like it was widening

This did not feel like one random moment to me. It felt connected to something I had already been noticing—the gap between you and the people who genuinely want to follow you closely has been getting wider.

That does not mean people stopped loving you or stopped believing in you. It means it has started taking more work to understand what is happening, where to find it and what each piece is supposed to mean.

Different doors, different stories

A person’s understanding of this rollout depended on where they found you.

Someone following Instagram was not necessarily seeing the same story as someone following TikTok, Twitter, YouTube or Discord.

And I do not mean every platform has to have the exact same post at the exact same time. Each platform should have its own purpose. But the larger story should still make sense no matter which door someone walks through.

Instead, it felt like different parts of the audience were being given different pieces without always being shown how those pieces belonged together.

Perception becomes dependent on the platform

If someone only sees one part, their opinion is going to be based on that part. So now the rollout is not only telling a story. The audience is filling in the missing pieces for themselves.

That is where confusion becomes perception, and perception can become harder to correct than the original problem.

Not rejection—exhaustion

People do not always announce when they are getting tired.

Fan fatigue does not always look like people dragging the song, unfollowing you or saying they are done. Sometimes they still like the post. They still support. They still care. They just stop trying to follow every piece.

They watch the first video and do not come back for the fourth. They see something new and assume they missed the context. They tell themselves they will catch up later, and then they never do.

That is why I keep separating hate from exhaustion. I do not think the core audience suddenly rejected you. I think some of them got tired of trying to understand the journey.

This is not always: “I do not like this.”
Sometimes it is: “I do not have the energy to keep figuring this out.”

Quiet disengagement is easy to miss

Because people are not always vocal about it, everything can look fine on the surface. But then the next thing happens and fewer people are emotionally present for it.

That is why I am not only looking at how one post performed. I am looking at whether each moment is making people more invested in the next one or slowly making them step back.

Two different conversations

The documentary and “Body So Tea” were not asking the audience to think about the same thing.

The documentary-style videos introduced bigger questions: Who are you as an artist? Where are you going? What are you trying to figure out? What does it mean that people keep seeing you as almost there?

Those are not small questions. Once you introduce them, people are going to watch whatever comes next like it is supposed to answer them.

But “Body So Tea” is playful. It is confident. It is a fun self-love record. It did not need to carry the weight of explaining your full artistic identity.

The problem was not that either one existed

The issue was placing them together in a way that made the audience expect the song to resolve a conversation it was never meant to resolve.

I think the deeper documentary would have made more sense as part of the larger journey into the next project, while “Body So Tea” could have been allowed to live inside a lighter, more playful world of its own.

So where was all this content coming from?

I was suspicious before I actually knew what was going on.

Because by this point, we were already fatigued with these clips. Then, all of a sudden, I started seeing even more of them being posted by random accounts.

Some of them were Coco-focused. Some of them had nothing to do with you. But they were all posting the same videos and spamming the clips everywhere.

Some of the clips we had already seen. But some of them, we had never seen before.

And I like to think I am pretty tuned in to the Coco side of the interwebs, so naturally I am sitting there like: Where is all this content coming from? Why do these random pages have clips that your fans have not seen? Why have these clips not been posted on your account? What is the source?

Because there was not one. The footage was just popping up on random accounts with no explanation.

“

Where is all this content coming from?

Then I found out it actually was a paid clip campaign

People were being given footage and instructions on how to frame it. The brief told them to make the clips feel like a documentary about an artist at a crossroads. It pushed the conversations about the label, the doubt, finding a new sound, stepping into a new era and connecting all of that back to Body So Tea.

So the heaviness of the rollout was not accidental. This was the conversation the campaign was intentionally telling people to keep pushing.

And I personally do not think that was the right conversation to platform this heavily. Your fans were already fatigued with this conversation, and then the paid strategy was to have random accounts continue spamming the same clips and the same narrative.

I do not think that was the right strategy for this rollout at all.

And then it became personal in a smaller way

I found a performance clip I had posted sitting inside the Week Two campaign files. I recognized the downloader filename because it was the same one I used.

I am not claiming ownership over a public performance. That is not my point. It was just another moment where I was like:

My reaction: Oh, okay. So this is happening again with your team and me.

I had already created my own Body So Tea fan engagement idea for people to participate in and post across social media. But after finding the campaign and seeing how everything was being pushed, I hit my own point of fatigue with the rollout.

I was like: Yeah. Y'all got this.

Not saying that you need me for anything. But I stopped wanting to do too much beyond posting and reposting what we already had, because the team clearly had the direction they wanted to take.

And then there was the AI

✧ throws hands up ✧

I am not going to try to explain your personal stance on generative AI. But before people know any nuance, the common assumption is that artists would want to protect their image, voice and likeness.

And some of these accounts were creating AI images of you for this rollout. I personally do not think that was necessary, especially because there had already been a little commotion when AI was used during the Christmas rollout.

So now, on top of everything else, somebody messier could have found this and turned it into: “Coco Jones is paying people to post her clips.” “Coco Jones is paying people to create AI images of her.”

Once it leaves the label and reaches the internet, it does not become, “Her label ran a paid clip campaign.” It becomes your name. They drag Coco Jones, even if paid campaigns are a normal strategy labels use.

That is why I think the strategy was risky. Not because paid campaigns exist, but because this specific one kept spamming a conversation people were already tired of, added AI images that were not needed and created room for your name to get dragged if the wrong person found it first.

The idea was not bad

I just think Body So Tea needed a different version of it.

I do not think the documentary was a bad idea. I actually think there was something there. I just do not think Body So Tea matched the way it was framed.

The rollout made the song carry this bigger conversation about your career, your sound and where you are going next, when the song itself could have just been fun.

And even if you wanted to keep the documentary style for this rollout, it did not have to feel like a career-shift documentary. It could have been about the song.

“

The documentary could still exist. It just needed to be about the world the song was actually living in.

Show us how Body So Tea became yours

Show us you hearing it for the first time. Show us recording it. Talk about why you liked it, why you changed the verses and where you wanted to add more of yourself to it.

Give us more behind the scenes from the photoshoot and the filming. Let the documentary follow the actual making of Body So Tea instead of making the song feel like the answer to every bigger question introduced before it.

People want to feel like they discovered the song

You cannot only start posting the song on release day and then ask TikTok to make it viral. Use it before the release as background music. Dance to it. Get ready to it. Let people see you actually living with the song.

That gives them a vibe and a moment they can recreate in their own way. It feels different from every post being framed as, “Here is my new single.”

And honestly, a lot of the content that came after the release is what I think the rollout could have looked like before it: the dances, the hair, the energy and you just having fun with the record.

Some people did not even know the rollout had started

Twitter is one of your least active platforms, but that is where the rollout began. So when I posted the actual song announcement, I saw people calling it a surprise drop and saying you were back.

They were happy, but they clearly had not seen the videos that were supposed to lead them there.

The first video reached one million views on Twitter and had a lot of interaction and discourse. The final video with the snippet also reached one million views, but the interaction was not the same. People seeing a number is not the same as people still being emotionally inside the story.

The deeper documentary could still become something bigger

I still think you should keep filming the journey to CJ2. Film the process while it is actually happening. Then, once you get to the end, sit down and decide what story you want to tell.

And instead of it only being staged conversations, include the fans. How did they feel during the process? What were they reacting to? What did they understand, and what did they miss?

Even now, you could still turn this around by including the reaction to Body So Tea as part of that larger film. Do not only look at the final outcome or whether a clip is doing well on Reels. There is so much happening underneath that, which is something I will get deeper into in another meeting.
